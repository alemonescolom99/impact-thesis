Beyond Investment

Not everything worth celebrating takes investment. These are B Corps, funds, and charities doing brilliant work, organizations I admire and think you should know about.

B Corp
Divine Chocolate

Divine was founded by cocoa farmers in 1998 and is still co-owned by the Kuapa Kokoo Farmers' Union in Ghana, 130,000 farmers who each own part of the brand. Every year, 20% of profits go back to the Union, which decides how to spend it themselves.

In an industry where the average cocoa farmer receives just 6% of the final cost of a chocolate bar, Divine flips the model. They also run literacy and numeracy programmes for farmers, 76% of participants are women, so they can negotiate contracts, run their businesses, and take leadership roles in their communities. Nearly 1,600 farmers have gone through the programme.

Why I love them

This is what stakeholder capitalism should actually look like. Not a slogan — farmers literally own the company and share in its success. The chocolate is also genuinely good, which helps.

Charity
Whale and Dolphin Conservation (WDC)

WDC is the leading global charity dedicated to protecting whales and dolphins. They work on ending captivity, stopping whaling, preventing deaths in fishing nets, tackling ocean pollution, and protecting habitats. They run the Scottish Dolphin Centre, coordinate citizen science projects like Shorewatch, and lobby governments worldwide.

Their vision is simple: a world where every whale and dolphin is safe and free.

Why I love them

I was supposed to sail to the Faroe Islands this year, but the trip had to turn back. I decided that if I couldn't make it there, something of mine still would — so I supported WDC's work in the region and adopted Coral, a humpback whale. What I admire about WDC is how they approach change: not through confrontation, but by working alongside local communities, supporting education, and fostering appreciation for whales as the intelligent, social beings they are. Real change comes from within.
